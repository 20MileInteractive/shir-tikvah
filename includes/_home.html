Inclusive

What does that really mean?

It means that we welcome and fully integrate everyone who wants to live a Jewish life or who is supportive of family members who do.

Everyone includes the whole megillah: Jews who have been Jewish from the start, families who light the menorah and trim the Christmas tree, folks who can remember their bat or bar mitzvah haftorah--and those who don’t know what a haftorah is. And included, of course, is the constellation of family structures and sexualities.

Inclusive also means that it’s okay if the size of our bank accounts differs considerably, because we make equal opportunity membership possible.

Joyous

At Shir Tikvah, joy can be big and loud, or intimate and quiet.

It’s ten teenagers making pizza before a youth group meeting.

It’s the deep, long breath taken when chanting a niggun—a repetitive melody without words before a prayer.

It’s a group of folks spending the day at a Habitat for Humanity worksite.

It’s two sets of grandparents, one Catholic, the other Jewish, when they hand the Torah to their grandchild at her bat mitzvah.

It’s the community welcoming a newborn at a Friday night service, and it’s the feeling that returns, with time, when remembering a loved one.

Community

We’re a bottom-up congregation, as opposed to top-down. We create our own programs; we reach a consensus before making decisions; the clergy and members are partners. Like a well functioning family, we are good humored and respectful of our differences - unless you’re a Yankee’s fan.

We also share a purpose—which is to create a living, breathing Jewish spiritual home that is generous of heart and hospitable to wherever we are - or whomever we accompany - on the Jewish journey. Making a community like this takes a lot of work and a lot of love, but there is nothing more worthwhile.
